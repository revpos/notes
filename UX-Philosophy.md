# UX Philosophy Behind Apps People Can't Stop Using

### 1. Smart Defaults

Allow the user to choose least possible choices in a form and
offer default values to the given choices as the best recommendations

Empty Forms create `Decision Fatigue`, i.e., inability to choose
options among mutiple choices.

> A blank form feels harder than a pre-filled one.

### 2. Goal Gradient Effect

The closer people feel to finishing something, the faster they move toward it.
Never start some input process(form, profile setup, etc) from zero.
Find something they have already done and show it as the current progress.
(NOTE: This seems like the In-Media-Res technique in filmmaking)

> 0% progress feels worse than 20%.

### 3. Reciprocity

Most apps ask you to sign up before they offer any value.

Sign up to see the results,
Create an account to continue,
Enter your email to unlock, etc

Its like a restaurant asking for the payment before you had your meal.

Imagine having a form you filled to expect some results but it asks you
to reveal your details for unlocking the results. Not a good UX.

But instead, if they offer a minimal TL;DR report and suggests you to
enter your email/sign up for a comprehensive breakdown of the results,
then it naturally feels likely to trade some details for the incoming value.

This is the reason why most businesses offer free samples, merch, etc.

Spotify offers 30-day free trial.
GCP offers $300 worth credits for free for sign up.
Lenskart offers free eye checkup for a visit, even without any purchase.

### 4. The `IKEA` Effect / Endownment Effect

People value something they invested in building than getting a work-done piece.

Duolingo does this with a first lesson even before sign up.
Their `Sign Up` button label is replaced with `Continue to Sign Up`
by the time they reach the end of the onboarding process showing
they are almost trading value offers to and fro.

This makes the user feel invested into the process of accomplishment
along with the previous techniques.

### 5. Loss Aversion

> The pain of losing something is psychologically twice as powerful as the
pleasure of gaining the same thing. - Daniel Kahneman (Nobel Prize Winner)

When offering new features, the user should feel the urge to adapt it
into his existing features as humans naturally tend to protect what they have
and if they won't adapt to change, notify the loss/threat as a consequence.

### 6. Contrast Effect

Imagine purchasing a costly product and it comes with invoice add-ons
like a MacBook Pro worth $1899 with Apple Care+ worth $49 /month (Just 2.6%).

This naturally feels extra investment with the absolute value ($49).
But this effect can be neutralized by giving a relative value like 2.6%

This makes the brain feel like doing no absolute evaluation anymore
but a relative evaluation.

Restaurants, Real-Estate, Salesmen use this technique starting to pitch
the initial deals at higher costs so they can negotiate and bring it down
later by the final quote to a relatively less cost and make it feel like
a better investment than the costly ones.


## Conclusion
All the above UX techniques serve the same purpose, share the same insight.
They help the users make logical decisions.

1. Defaults feel like secure recommendations.
2. A progress, even a fake one, creates real momentum.
3. A gift feels a debt.
4. Building something feels rewarding, makes it yours.
5. Not upgrading almost feels like a loss/threat.
6. The first number sets the anchor but the final one in contrast feels best.
